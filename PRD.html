Executive Summary

PetSehat is Indonesia's first vet-backed pet ecommerce platform — built on the belief that pet owners make better decisions, and buy with more confidence, when real veterinary knowledge is woven into every part of the shopping experience. We sell food, health products, and everyday supplies for cats, dogs, rabbits, and more, and we back every product page with free, expert-reviewed guidance so that a first-time cat owner in Jakarta and a seasoned multi-pet household in Surabaya both feel equally supported. Our model is straightforward: free vet advice earns trust, trust drives the first purchase, and a great first purchase earns the second — and the tenth. Margin on goods sustains the business; the advice is how we earn the right to be there. By delivering a fast, responsive web experience that works equally well on a phone in the waiting room and a desktop at home, paired with local e-wallet payments and the repeat purchase behaviours that matter most to Indonesian pet owners, PetSehat is positioned to become the single most trusted place to care for your animal in the country.

Problem Statement

Fragmented, uncurated supply

Pet care products in Indonesia are scattered across wet markets, general Tokopedia and Shopee listings, and neighbourhood pet shops with wildly inconsistent stock. There is no specialist channel that curates products by quality, safety, or veterinary relevance. Pet owners are left to guess.

No trustworthy answer before buying

When a new dog owner wants to know which food suits their puppy's breed and age, there is nowhere online to get a confident, expert-backed answer in the same place they shop. Search results return brand marketing. Forums return conflicting opinions. The gap between "I have a question" and "I feel safe buying this" is wide and filled with anxiety.

Vets are trusted but not built for retail

Vet clinics are where Indonesian pet owners go when they want a straight answer — but clinics are not set up for convenient retail. Product selection is narrow, prices are high, and there is no ability to browse, compare, or repurchase online. The most trusted voice in the category is completely absent from ecommerce.

Auto-replenish does not exist for pets

Pet food, flea treatment, and supplements are recurring needs — but no dedicated pet platform in Indonesia offers subscription or auto-replenish. Owners either forget and run out, or over-order to compensate. A frictionless way to set and forget routine purchases simply does not exist in the category.

Market & Competitive Landscape

Indonesia Pet Market Context

72 million pet-owning households nationwide, with penetration highest in Java and growing rapidly in tier-2 cities. The pet care market is expanding at approximately 15% year-on-year, driven by younger urban owners treating pets as family members rather than livestock.

Indonesian internet users are mobile-first, with mid-range Android devices on 4G as the most common access mode — but desktop remains a meaningful surface for considered purchases, research-heavy sessions (comparing foods, reading vet content), and buyers shopping from work or home. GoPay, OVO, Dana, and QRIS together account for the large majority of digital consumer transactions — bank cards lag significantly. Any product built for this market must deliver a first-class responsive experience across phone and desktop, and treat e-wallets as the payment baseline, not an enhancement.

The Gap in the Market

There is no trusted, mobile-first, vet-curated pet specialist operating in Indonesia today. The category is either too generic (Tokopedia/Shopee) or too inaccessible (vet clinics). PetSehat occupies a position no competitor currently holds.

The US market provides a useful proof of concept: Chewy built a multi-billion-dollar business on specialist curation, subscription, and genuine customer care in the pet category. The model translates — Indonesia is earlier in the cycle, which means the opportunity is larger for a first mover who gets the trust layer right.

Value Proposition & Differentiator

PetSehat's core promise: shop with the confidence of a vet by your side — for free, every time. We are not a generic store that happens to sell pet products. We are the place where every buying decision is informed by real veterinary knowledge, available to every pet owner regardless of budget or experience.

Why free advice is the right model

It converts. A pet owner who finds the answer to "which food is right for my senior dog?" on PetSehat is far more likely to buy that food from PetSehat than to go back to a generic platform. Advice removes the final hesitation before purchase. This is not charity — it is the most effective conversion mechanism in the category.

It builds lasting loyalty. Trust compounds. A customer who came for advice and had a great experience does not need to be re-acquired next month. They return because PetSehat is where they feel understood. This is how we reach a 40%+ repeat purchase rate — not through discounts, but through genuine expertise.

Why this beats the alternatives

Tokopedia and Shopee cannot replicate it easily. Generic marketplaces are structurally unable to offer consistent vet-backed guidance at the product level. They have thousands of sellers, no quality control, and no editorial voice. Curation is a moat they cannot build without becoming a different kind of business.

It does not add revenue complexity. Charging for advice would require a payments flow, a scheduling system, regulatory sign-off, and a very different customer expectation to manage. Free advice is operationally simple: we produce it, we publish it, it works. The monetisation happens downstream at the product level where the margin already exists.

It is sustainable. The cost of producing vet-reviewed content is a fixed operational cost that does not scale linearly with transactions. As order volume grows, the advice cost per order falls — while the conversion and retention benefit it delivers remains constant.

Business Model

Direct retail — own inventory, own margin

PetSehat is a direct retailer. We purchase inventory from suppliers and distributors, hold it in our own fulfilment infrastructure, and sell it at a retail price we control. There are no third-party sellers, no marketplace commission structures, and no revenue sharing. This model gives us full authority over product quality, pricing, stock reliability, and the customer experience from browse to delivery.

Where revenue comes from

Product sales margin only. Revenue equals the difference between what we pay suppliers and what customers pay us. There is no marketplace take rate, no subscription fee, no advice fee, and no premium tier. Simplicity is deliberate — one clean revenue line is easier to operate and easier for customers to trust.

Subscription (auto-replenish) orders are included in product revenue. The subscription mechanic increases order frequency and reduces churn; it does not carry a separate fee. The value to the customer is convenience; the value to PetSehat is predictable, recurring revenue and lower re-acquisition cost.

The unit economics logic

Free advice justifies itself through LTV. Consider the chain: a user arrives with a question, gets a trustworthy answer on PetSehat, buys the product we recommended, has a good experience, and returns next month. That single advice interaction has acquired a customer who may spend across dozens of orders over several years.

Compared to paid acquisition — influencer campaigns, performance ads, promotional discounts — the cost of producing vet content is highly competitive on a cost-per-retained-customer basis. Advice is not a cost centre; it is a low-cost, high-return acquisition and retention channel embedded in the product itself.

The flywheel: Free advice builds trust. Trust drives conversion. Conversion funds inventory and content. Better inventory and content attracts more pet owners. More pet owners generate more data for personalisation. Better personalisation drives repeat purchase. Repeat purchase is the North Star. Everything reinforces everything else.

Success Metrics

North Star Metric

90-Day Repeat Purchase Rate — the percentage of first-time buyers who place a second order within 90 days of their initial purchase. This single metric best represents whether PetSehat is genuinely trusted as a recurring source of pet health products, not just a one-time destination. Target: 40%+ by Month 6.

Measured by: cohort analysis on order timestamps per user ID. A user enters the cohort on their first completed order date. The metric fires when a second completed order exists within the 90-day window.

Scope — v1 Features

PetSehat v1 is a frontend-only implementation. All features listed below are fully designed and specified as a production product, but run on mock and static data. Each feature includes a mock implementation note describing the v1 approach. Real backend integration is scoped to Phase 2.
P0 1. Catalog & Browse

Product listing pages organized by category: Food & Nutrition, Health & Meds (OTC only), and Supplies & Accessories. Users can browse by pet type (dog, cat, rabbit, bird, fish), filter by brand, price range, and rating. Category landing pages include vet-curated featured collections and seasonal highlights.

v1 Mock: Product catalog is a static JSON file (src/data/products.json) containing ~60 representative SKUs across all three categories. Category pages read and render from this file at build time. No inventory levels, stock status, or pricing logic is live — all values are hardcoded in the JSON.
P0 2. Search & Filters

Keyword search across product names, descriptions, and vet article titles. Faceted filters let users narrow results by pet type, category, brand, price range, and rating. Search results page shows result count, active filter chips with one-tap removal, and a sort selector (relevance, price low–high, price high–low, newest, most reviewed).

v1 Mock: Search and all filters are implemented entirely client-side using JavaScript array methods against the static product JSON. There is no search index or backend query. Relevance sorting is simulated by product order in the JSON file. Performance is acceptable for a catalog of under 100 items.
P0 3. Pet Profiles

Users can create up to 5 pet profiles per account. Each profile captures: pet name, species, breed, age (in months or years), weight (kg), sex, spayed/neutered status, and open-text health notes. Profiles are the personalization engine — product recommendations, vet article suggestions, and subscription reminders all reference the active pet profile. Users can switch the active profile from any page via a persistent avatar chip in the header.

v1 Mock: Pet profiles are created and stored entirely in localStorage under the key petsehat_profiles. Recommendation logic is a simple client-side lookup: species and age bracket map to a curated subset of the static product catalog. No ML or server-side personalization is involved.
P0 4. Vet-Backed Advice

A content hub featuring: vet-written articles organized by topic (nutrition, preventive care, common symptoms, breed guides), curated product recommendation guides authored by vets, an interactive symptom checker (select pet type → symptoms → recommended action and OTC products), and an "Ask Our Vet" async form where users submit a question and receive a confirmation that a vet will respond. This is the primary trust differentiator and is surfaced throughout the product — on the home page, product detail pages, and at checkout.

v1 Mock: All article and guide content is static markdown or JSON rendered at build time. The symptom checker is a client-side decision tree (static symptom-tree.json). The "Ask Our Vet" form submits to a local state handler and displays a success screen — no email, no ticket, no backend. Vet bylines and credentials are representative placeholder data.
P0 5. Product Detail Pages

Each product page includes: a photo gallery (swipeable on mobile), full product description, ingredients or specifications, a vet note callout explaining why this product is recommended and for which pets, customer reviews with star rating and pet-type filter, a "Frequently Bought Together" section, quantity selector, and add-to-cart CTA. Products flagged as vet-recommended display a badge throughout the catalog.

v1 Mock: All product data (photos, descriptions, vet notes, specs) is pulled from the static product JSON. Reviews are a hardcoded array of 4–8 sample reviews per product. "Frequently Bought Together" items are manually curated associations in the JSON. Product images are sourced from Unsplash with static URLs.
P0 6. Cart & Checkout

Persistent cart accessible from any page. Users can adjust quantities, remove items, apply a promo or coupon code, and see a running total with delivery estimate. Checkout flow: delivery address entry (with saved addresses for logged-in users) → delivery method selection → payment method selection → order review → confirm. A progress indicator shows the user which step they are on throughout checkout.

v1 Mock: Cart state is persisted in localStorage under petsehat_cart. Promo codes are validated against a hardcoded list of valid codes in client-side logic (e.g., PETSEHAT10 for 10% off). Delivery fees are a static lookup by courier. The checkout flow is a multi-step UI with no real order submission — the final step writes a mock order to localStorage and navigates to a success screen.
P0 7. Payments

Payment method selection screen supports: GoPay, OVO, Dana, QRIS (scan-to-pay), and Bank Transfer / Virtual Account (BCA, Mandiri, BNI, BRI). Each method shows its logo, a brief instruction, and estimated confirmation time. After selecting a method the user sees a payment instruction screen (e.g., QR code image for QRIS, VA number for bank transfer) with a countdown timer and a "I have paid" CTA that advances the flow.

v1 Mock: No real payment gateway is integrated. Payment method logos and instruction screens are static UI. The QR code is a static placeholder image. After tapping "I have paid," the app immediately transitions to an order confirmed screen — no webhook, no polling, no real transaction. Payment status in mock order history is always "Paid."
P1 8. Fulfillment & Courier Tracking

At checkout, users choose a courier from JNE, J&T Express, or SiCepat. Each courier displays estimated delivery days and shipping cost. Post-purchase, an order detail page shows a tracking timeline: Order Confirmed → Packed → Picked Up by Courier → In Transit → Out for Delivery → Delivered. Users receive an estimated delivery date displayed prominently on the order page.

v1 Mock: Courier options and pricing are static lookup tables. The tracking timeline is simulated: mock orders cycle through status stages based on a hardcoded mock timeline (e.g., "Packed" 1 day after order, "In Transit" day 2, "Delivered" day 4). Tracking numbers are randomly generated strings. No real courier API is called.
P0 9. Accounts & Order History

User registration (name, email, phone, password) and login. Profile page shows: account details, saved delivery addresses, linked pet profiles, and order history. Order history lists past orders with status, items, and total. Each order is tappable to an order detail page with the tracking timeline. Logged-in state persists across sessions.

v1 Mock: Authentication is fully mocked. A hardcoded mock user (demo@petsehat.id / any password) unlocks the logged-in state stored in localStorage. Registration form submits to a success screen and creates a mock session. Order history is a hardcoded array of 3–5 sample past orders. No real auth provider or database is used.
P0 10. Subscriptions / Auto-Replenish

Users can subscribe to any eligible product (primarily food and OTC health items) and set a delivery frequency: every 2 weeks, monthly, or every 2 months. The subscription setup flow confirms the first delivery date, payment method, and delivery address. An active subscriptions page lets users pause, change frequency, or cancel. The home page surfaces a personalized "Upcoming Delivery" widget for subscribed users.

v1 Mock: Subscription creation writes to localStorage under petsehat_subscriptions. The subscription management page reads and mutates this local state. No recurring billing, no scheduled job, and no real future orders are created. The "Upcoming Delivery" date is calculated client-side from the subscription creation date and chosen frequency. Pause and cancel update local state only.
P1 11. Ratings & Reviews

Users can leave a review on any purchased product: star rating (1–5), written review (minimum 20 characters), optional photo upload, and pet type tag (so other shoppers can filter by relevant pet). Reviews are surfaced on product detail pages with filters for rating and pet type. An aggregate rating and review count appear on product cards throughout the catalog.

v1 Mock: Existing reviews are static data in the product JSON. The review submission form validates input client-side and on submit displays a "Thank you for your review" success screen — the new review is not actually appended to any persistent store (it disappears on page refresh). Photo upload accepts a file but does not transmit it anywhere. Aggregate ratings are pre-computed static values in the product JSON.

Key User Journeys

These three journeys represent the highest-value flows in v1. Each step maps to a concrete UI screen or interaction. Design and engineering should treat these as the primary acceptance paths for QA and usability testing.

Journey 1: Discovery → Vet Advice → First Purchase

Persona: Budi, 29, new cat owner in Jakarta. First visit to PetSehat. Primary need: find the right food for his 8-month-old kitten.

  1. Home page. Budi lands on the PetSehat home page. He sees a vet-curated hero banner: "Not sure what to feed your kitten? Our vets have you covered." A prominent CTA reads "Find food for my cat."
  2. Pet profile creation. Tapping the CTA opens a lightweight profile creation modal. Budi enters: species (cat), name (Mochi), breed (Domestic Shorthair), age (8 months), weight (3.2 kg). He saves the profile. This is his first interaction with personalization.
  3. Personalized recommendations. He is taken to a curated product listing page: "Recommended for Mochi." The page shows kitten-appropriate food and health products filtered by Mochi's species and age bracket, each tagged with a vet-approved badge.
  4. Vet article. A content card between rows reads "Why protein content matters for kittens under 12 months." Budi taps it and reads a short vet-authored article (2–3 minute read). At the bottom, the article links back to three specific products with an explanation of why each is appropriate.
  5. Product detail page. Budi taps Royal Canin Kitten Pouch. He reads the description, sees the vet note ("High protein and DHA for brain development — ideal for kittens up to 12 months"), swipes through the photo gallery, and reads four reviews — he filters to "Cat owners" reviews.
  6. Add to cart. Budi selects quantity 3 and taps "Tambah ke Keranjang." A confirmation drawer slides up showing the item in cart with a subtotal. He also adds a recommended dental treat from the "Frequently Bought Together" section.
  7. Checkout — address. Budi proceeds to checkout. He enters his delivery address: Jakarta Selatan, 12930. He selects J&T Express (2–3 days, Rp 18.000).
  8. Checkout — payment. He selects GoPay as his payment method and sees the GoPay payment instruction screen with a deeplink to open GoPay and a "I have paid" button.
  9. Order confirmation. Tapping "I have paid" transitions to the order confirmation screen: order number, estimated delivery date, and a prompt to "Set up auto-replenish so Mochi never runs out." Budi taps "Maybe later" for now.
v1 Mock: Profile is stored in localStorage. Recommendations are a static filter. The vet article is static content. Cart persists in localStorage. Checkout is a multi-step UI ending in a success screen. No real order is placed. GoPay screen shows a static placeholder.

Journey 2: Repeat Replenishment via Subscription

Persona: Sari, 34, returning customer in Surabaya. Has been buying Whiskas monthly. Wants to simplify her routine with auto-replenish.

  1. Re-engagement entry. Sari returns to PetSehat 3 weeks after her last order. The home page shows a personalized "Time to reorder?" widget: "Whiskas Adult Tuna 1.2 kg — your usual order. Estimated to run out in 5 days." A single CTA: "Reorder Now."
  2. Subscription setup prompt. Tapping "Reorder Now" adds the item to cart and shows a bottom sheet: "Never run out again. Subscribe and get 5% off every delivery." Options: Every 2 weeks / Monthly / Every 2 months. Sari selects Monthly.
  3. Subscription confirmation. She confirms the subscription: first delivery on her selected date, same address as last order (pre-filled), OVO as payment method. She reviews and taps "Start Subscription."

Technical Approach & Constraints (v1: Frontend-Only Mock)

What "Frontend-Only Mock" Means

In Phase 1, PetSehat runs entirely inside the user's browser. There is no backend server, no database, no real payment processing, and no live API. Every screen, flow, and interaction is powered by static data and browser-native storage. A person using the app sees a fully interactive, visually complete product — but behind the scenes, nothing persists to a server and no money changes hands.

This is a deliberate product strategy, not a technical limitation. It lets the team validate the most important unknowns (does vet-backed trust convert? do pet profiles feel worth filling in? does the subscription upsell land?) before spending months building a production backend.

Data Layer

All data that a real backend would serve is instead defined as static JSON files or TypeScript arrays within the codebase. This includes the product catalog (names, prices, images, descriptions, category tags), pet profiles (species, breed, age, health notes), vet advice articles, order history, and subscription plans. There are no API calls in v1. Components import data directly or read from a local data module.

The data shapes used in Phase 1 must match the API response shapes planned for Phase 2. This is non-negotiable: if the mock uses a product.price field as a number in IDR cents, the real API must return the same field with the same semantics. Divergence here forces UI rewrites in Phase 2.

Subscriptions and auto-replenish The full subscription setup flow is interactive: users pick a product, select a frequency (weekly/monthly), and confirm. The subscription is saved to localStorage. The subscription management screen reads and renders this data. No recurring charge logic runs.
Vet advice and "Ask a Vet" Vet articles are static content. The "Ask our vet" form collects the user's question and displays a confirmation message ("Pertanyaanmu sudah kami terima — tim dokter hewan kami akan menghubungimu dalam 1×24 jam"). No message is sent anywhere.
User authentication Login and registration forms are rendered as real UI. On submit, a mock user object is written to sessionStorage and the user is shown as logged in for the session. No passwords are verified.

Tech Stack

Core TanStack Start — React 19 with file-based routing via TanStack Router, server-rendered through Vite and Nitro. Chosen for its first-class TypeScript support, fast HMR, and the clean separation between route files that mirrors how feature teams will think about pages.

Core Tailwind CSS v4 — utility-first styling with the design token system (coral primary, honey yellow accent, Geist and Fraunces typefaces) defined as CSS custom properties and surfaced through Tailwind's @theme. Keeps styling consistent and refactorable.

Core shadcn/ui (new-york, Base UI) — accessible, unstyled-first component primitives that compose with Tailwind. Used for all interactive UI (dialogs, sheets, tabs, selects, forms), with responsive variants where the pattern differs by viewport (e.g., a bottom Sheet on mobile, a side Dialog on desktop). Never replaced with custom components where shadcn has an equivalent.

Tooling Biome 2.x — linting and formatting in a single tool. Enforces tabs, double quotes, and import organization. Runs in CI to prevent style drift.

Why This Approach De-Risks the Project

Building a full ecommerce backend before validating the core product hypothesis is an expensive bet. PetSehat's core hypothesis is that vet-backed trust, not price or selection, drives repeat purchase in the Indonesian pet health market. A frontend mock lets the team test that hypothesis with real users in weeks, not months.

Specifically, this approach lets the product team run usability tests on the full end-to-end flow; gives the vet content team a real surface to review and critique; allows the business team to demo to investors with a working product; and gives engineering a clear, tested UX target before writing a line of backend code. The risk of "building the wrong thing" is dramatically reduced before any infrastructure cost is committed.

Mock-to-Real Migration Path

When Phase 2 begins, the migration from mock to real follows a layered replacement strategy — not a rewrite.

Step 1 — Data layer: Replace static JSON imports with API client calls (e.g., fetchProducts() instead of import products from './data/products.json'). Because the data shapes are identical, component code does not change.

Step 2 — Auth: Replace sessionStorage mock auth with a real JWT/session flow. The login and registration UI already exists and just needs its submit handlers wired to real endpoints.

Step 3 — Cart and orders: Replace localStorage cart with server-side cart (linked to authenticated user). Order completion flow calls the real order API instead of writing to local storage.

Step 4 — Payments: Wire payment method selection screens to the Midtrans SDK. The UI screens are already built; they just need real transaction tokens from the backend.

Step 5 — Analytics: Replace console-log event stubs with real Segment/Mixpanel SDK calls. Event names and properties are already defined and standardized in Phase 1.

No page-level UI rewrites are required if Phase 1 is built to the data contracts above.

What Must Be Production-Quality Even in the Mock

The following are not "we'll fix it later" items. They must meet real-product standards from the first day of development:

  • Performance: LCP under 2.5s, bundle under 500KB gzipped. Measured with Lighthouse on a simulated mid-range Android device in Chrome DevTools before any merge to main.
  • Accessibility: WCAG 2.1 AA. Every new component ships with proper ARIA attributes, keyboard navigation, and color contrast validation.
  • Responsive UX: Every screen designed and tested across the full range — mobile (360px), tablet, and desktop (1280px+). No horizontal overflow at any width, no hover-only states, no tap targets smaller than 44px, and desktop layouts that use the extra space deliberately rather than stretching a mobile layout.
  • Analytics event stubs: Every meaningful user interaction fires a structured event to the analytics stub. The event taxonomy is defined in the metrics section and must be followed exactly.
v1 Mock: This entire section describes the v1 architecture. The mock is the Phase 1 deliverable. All flows documented in this PRD are built as interactive browser experiences backed by static data and localStorage. The word "mock" does not mean low-fidelity — it means no server. Visual quality, UX quality, performance, and accessibility are held to production standards throughout.

Open Questions

The following questions are unresolved at the time of this PRD. Each must have a decision before or during the Phase 2 build. Leaving them open past the deadlines indicated creates downstream design, legal, or engineering risk.

  1. Vet sourcing model: in-house employees or freelance contributors? An in-house vet team gives content quality control and exclusivity but adds significant headcount cost. A freelance model is cheaper and faster to spin up but harder to standardize and retain. Which model do we use at launch, and what is the minimum viable vet team size to seed the content library?
    Decision owner: CEO / Head of Content. Deadline: Month 1.
  2. Content localization depth: full Bahasa Indonesia content or bilingual from day one? Writing all vet content natively in Bahasa Indonesia is the right choice for the primary audience, but it constrains which vet contributors can write for us. Should we accept English drafts and translate, or require Indonesian-language production throughout?
    Decision owner: Head of Content. Deadline: Month 2.
  3. Courier partner selection: which carriers do we integrate first, and how do we handle multi-carrier fulfillment? JNE, SiCepat, J&T, and AnterAja all have different coverage maps, rate structures, and API quality. Do we negotiate direct contracts or use an aggregator API (e.g., Shipper, Biteship) to abstract carrier selection?
    Decision owner: Head of Operations. Deadline: Month 4 (before Phase 2 fulfillment build begins).
  4. Mock-to-real data migration strategy: how do we handle Phase 1 test users who want to carry their data into Phase 2? If usability testers create pet profiles and "orders" in the mock, do we offer a way to migrate their data into real accounts when Phase 2 launches? This affects both the technical architecture and the onboarding email strategy.
    Decision owner: Product Lead + Engineering Lead. Deadline: Month 2.
  5. Pricing strategy vs. Tokopedia and Shopee storefronts: do we compete on price, or hold margin and compete on trust? Most pet products are already available on Tokopedia, often at thin-margin prices. PetSehat's vet-trust positioning justifies a small premium, but we need a defined pricing policy before catalog pricing begins. What is our target gross margin floor per category?
    Decision owner: CEO / Head of Commercial. Deadline: Month 3.
  6. COD timeline: when exactly in Phase 2 do we launch Cash on Delivery, and for which cities? COD is deferred from v1 but is a real barrier for a meaningful portion of Indonesian shoppers. Delaying it too long will suppress conversion in Phase 2. We need a specific month and city list, not "later."
    Decision owner: Head of Operations. Deadline: Month 3.
  7. UU PDP legal review: do we need a Data Protection Officer (DPO) and when must one be appointed? UU No. 27/2022 may require the appointment of a DPO depending on the volume and sensitivity of personal data processed. This needs a formal legal opinion before Phase 2 collects real user data.
    Decision owner: CEO + Legal Counsel. Deadline: Month 2.
  8. Subscription payment failure handling: what happens when an auto-replenish charge fails? E-wallet balances can be insufficient at the time of a recurring charge. Do we retry automatically, notify the user and pause, or cancel the subscription? The policy affects both the backend payment flow design and the user-facing subscription management UI.
    Decision owner: Product Lead + Head of Payments. Deadline: Month 4.
  9. Search architecture: Typesense (self-hosted) or Algolia (SaaS) for Phase 2? Both support Indonesian language tokenization and typo tolerance. Typesense is open-source and cheaper at scale; Algolia is faster to integrate and has better managed infrastructure. The choice affects both Phase 2 build time and ongoing infrastructure cost.
    Decision owner: Engineering Lead. Deadline: Month 3.
  10. Loyalty and rewards program design: should a basic rewards mechanic launch with Phase 2 or be deferred to Phase 3? A points-for-purchase system launched alongside the real store could accelerate the repeat purchase rate from day one. But it adds design, backend, and legal complexity (points can be considered a financial instrument under certain Indonesian regulations). Does the expected retention lift justify the Phase 2 scope addition?
    Decision owner: CEO / Head of Growth. Deadline: Month 4.

Appendix: Glossary & Assumptions

Glossary

Term Definition
OTC Over-the-Counter. Medications and health products that can be purchased without a veterinary prescription. PetSehat's Phase 1 and Phase 2 catalog is OTC-only.
Rx Prescription. Medications that legally require a licensed veterinarian's prescription before dispensing. Deferred to Phase 3 due to the regulatory and verification workflow complexity involved.
AOV Average Order Value. Total revenue from completed orders divided by the number of completed orders in a given period. A key indicator of basket health and upsell effectiveness.
LTV Lifetime Value (or Customer Lifetime Value, CLV). The total net revenue a customer is expected to generate over their entire relationship with PetSehat. Drives decisions about customer acquisition cost ceilings.
NPS Net Promoter Score. A customer loyalty metric derived from asking users "How likely are you to recommend PetSehat to a friend?" on a 0–10 scale. Promoters (9–10) minus Detractors (0–6) as a percentage of total respondents.
GMV Gross Merchandise Value. The total value of all goods sold through the platform before deducting returns, discounts, or platform costs. A top-line volume indicator.
MAU Monthly Active Users. The count of distinct users who have at least one recorded session within a calendar month. The primary measure of platform reach and engagement breadth.
PWA Progressive Web App. A web application that uses modern browser APIs to deliver app-like experiences: installability to the home screen, offline support via service workers, and push notifications. PetSehat targets PWA compliance from Phase 1.
LCP Largest Contentful Paint. A Core Web Vitals metric that measures when the largest visible content element in the viewport (typically a hero image or headline) finishes rendering. Target: under 2.5 seconds.
CLS Cumulative Layout Shift. A Core Web Vitals metric measuring unexpected visual movement of page content during load. High CLS creates a jarring experience where buttons or links move as the user attempts to tap them.
VA (Virtual Account) Virtual Account. A bank-issued temporary account number used for one-time payments in Indonesia. The user transfers the exact order amount to the VA number within a time window. Widely used for users without e-wallets.
QRIS Quick Response Code Indonesian Standard. Bank Indonesia's national QR payment standard that unifies GoPay, OVO, Dana, ShopeePay, and bank mobile apps into a single scannable code. Launched nationally in 2019 and widely adopted in retail and online commerce.
UU PDP Undang-Undang Perlindungan Data Pribadi (Personal Data Protection Law), Indonesian Law No. 27 of 2022. Governs how personal data of Indonesian residents may be collected, processed, and stored. Came into full effect in 2024.
Auto-replenish / Subscription A recurring purchase arrangement where the user authorizes PetSehat to automatically charge and ship a selected product on a defined schedule (e.g., every 30 days). Used interchangeably in this PRD. Designed to drive repeat purchase rate and reduce customer churn on consumable products like pet food and flea treatments.

Assumptions

This PRD is built on the following assumptions. If any of these prove false, the relevant sections should be revisited before Phase 2 build begins.

  1. Indonesian pet owners shop across both phones and desktops — phones for quick and on-the-go purchases, desktops for research-heavy and considered ones. A responsive web app (no native app install barrier) reaches the full addressable market across both surfaces.
  2. GoPay, OVO, and Dana have sufficient penetration among smartphone-owning pet owners in Tier 1 and Tier 2 Indonesian cities to make e-wallet-first payment viable at launch. COD can be deferred without blocking the first meaningful buyer cohort.
  3. Vet-backed product recommendations and health content represent a genuine, unmet trust gap in the current Indonesian pet product market — one that mainstream platforms (Tokopedia, Shopee) are not positioned to fill credibly.
  4. Licensed Indonesian veterinarians (drh.) are willing to contribute content to a commercial platform in exchange for reasonable compensation and visibility, and can be sourced within the Phase 1 timeline.
  5. The primary pet categories at launch (dogs and cats) account for the large majority of addressable spend. Exotic pet categories (reptiles, birds, fish) can be deferred without materially limiting the initial market.
  6. A frontend-only mock with realistic flows and real-looking UI is sufficient to generate valid user behavior signal in usability testing. Users will engage with the mock as if it were a real purchasing environment.
  7. TanStack Start (React 19 + Vite + Nitro) is a stable enough foundation for a production ecommerce frontend. No migration to a different framework will be needed between Phase 1 and Phase 2.
  8. Midtrans is the appropriate payment gateway for Phase 2 given its Indonesian market coverage, GoPay/OVO/Dana integrations, and virtual account support. No significant integration risk is assumed.
  9. Indonesian pet owners are increasingly comfortable purchasing health-related pet products online, including OTC medications and supplements. Regulatory barriers to OTC online pet health product sales in Indonesia will not materially change before Phase 2 launch.
  10. The 90-day repeat purchase rate is a leading indicator of LTV and brand loyalty in this category. Optimizing for this metric will directionally improve all other business outcomes including GMV and subscription attach rate.
  11. PetSehat will not face material intellectual property or brand conflict issues with the name, domain, or visual identity in the Indonesian market. Legal clearance is assumed to have been completed or will be completed before Phase 2 launch.
